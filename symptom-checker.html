Common Sports Injuries Quick Reference

Explore the most frequently diagnosed orthopedic sports injuries, their severity grading, affected anatomy, and average clinical recovery windows.

Severe

ACL Tear (Anterior Cruciate Ligament)

Complete or partial rupture of the knee's primary stabilizing ligament, typically caused by sudden pivoting, deceleration, or landing from jumps. Often accompanied by an audible "pop" and immediate swelling.

Affected Joint Knee
Recovery Window 6–9 Months
Treatment Surgical Reconstruction
Moderate

Rotator Cuff Impingement

Compression and irritation of the rotator cuff tendons beneath the acromion bone during overhead motions. Common in swimmers, baseball pitchers, and tennis players. Can progress to tears if untreated.

Affected Joint Shoulder
Recovery Window 6–12 Weeks
Treatment Physical Therapy + Injection
Severe

Achilles Tendon Rupture

Complete tear of the Achilles tendon connecting the calf muscle to the heel bone. Typically occurs during explosive push-off movements like sprinting or jumping. Requires immediate immobilization and usually surgical repair.

Affected Joint Ankle & Foot
Recovery Window 4–6 Months
Treatment Surgical Suture Repair
Mild

Tennis Elbow (Lateral Epicondylitis)

Overuse injury causing micro-tearing of the forearm extensor tendons at the lateral epicondyle. Triggered by repetitive gripping and wrist extension activities. Responds well to eccentric strengthening exercises.

Affected Joint Elbow
Recovery Window 6–12 Weeks
Treatment Rest + Physical Therapy
Moderate

Herniated Lumbar Disc

A disc nucleus pushes through a tear in the outer annulus, compressing adjacent spinal nerves. Causes radiating leg pain (sciatica), numbness, and weakness. Often manageable with conservative care but may need surgical decompression.

Affected Area Lumbar Spine
Recovery Window 8–16 Weeks
Treatment Therapy / Microdiscectomy
Moderate

Hip Labral Tear

Tear in the ring of cartilage (labrum) surrounding the hip socket. Common in athletes performing deep squats, pivoting, or twisting. Causes deep groin pain and clicking sensation. Often requires arthroscopic surgical repair.

Affected Joint Hip
Recovery Window 12–16 Weeks
Treatment Arthroscopic Repair
Urgency Guide

When Should You See a Specialist?

Not every injury requires surgery. Use this clinical triage guide to understand when self-care is sufficient, when to schedule a consultation, and when emergency medical care is critical.

Emergency — Seek Immediate Care

Go to the nearest emergency room or call 911 immediately if you experience any of these symptoms after a sports injury.

  • Visible bone deformity or open fracture
  • Complete loss of motor function in a limb
  • Severe, uncontrollable bleeding at injury site
  • Loss of sensation, numbness, or tingling below the injury
  • Joint locked in position and cannot be moved

Urgent — Book a Consultation

Schedule an appointment within 48–72 hours if you experience persistent symptoms that are not resolving with basic R.I.C.E. treatment.

  • Swelling that persists beyond 48 hours
  • Joint instability or "giving way" feeling
  • Audible popping sound at time of injury
  • Pain that limits weight-bearing or daily activities
  • Recurring pain in the same joint over several weeks

Self-Care — Monitor at Home

Many minor sports injuries respond well to the R.I.C.E. protocol (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) and typically resolve within 1–2 weeks.

  • Mild soreness after physical activity
  • Minor bruising without joint instability
  • Stiffness that improves with gentle movement
  • Muscle cramps or delayed-onset muscle soreness
  • Slight swelling that reduces with ice within 24 hours
